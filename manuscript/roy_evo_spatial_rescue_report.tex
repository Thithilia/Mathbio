\title{Eco-Evolutionary Rescue and Basin Structure in a Roy-Style Predator--Prey Model}
\author{Mathbio research notes}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We study whether spatial structure can alter indirect evolutionary rescue in a predator--prey model with evolving prey defense. A fixed-defense spatial control did not produce robust predator persistence beyond the well-mixed reference. Introducing prey defense evolution in the well-mixed model increased the predator mortality stress tolerated by the system, consistent with indirect evolutionary rescue through a decline in defense frequency. In the spatial eco-evolutionary model, however, persistence was not organized by a single monotone stress threshold. Multi-horizon, continuation, and basin analyses showed path-dependent dynamics and bistability: at the same mortality stress, predator-persistent and predator-extinct outcomes were both reachable from different initial conditions. A focused basin-boundary scan showed that initial defense frequency and predator abundance influence basin entry. Subsequent ODE-PDE comparison showed that this basin dependence is primarily reaction-dominated: representative PDE solutions remained nearly homogeneous and matched ODE trajectories from the same initial means. These results suggest that spatially extended eco-evolutionary rescue should be analyzed in terms of reachable outcome basins rather than scalar persistence thresholds, while spatial-pattern-mediated rescue is not currently supported in this tested parameterization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Evolutionary rescue occurs when adaptive trait or genotype-frequency change allows a population to persist after environmental stress \citep{GomulkiewiczHolt1995,BellGonzalez2009,Bell2017}. In the present predator--prey setting, the relevant trait is prey defense. Higher defense reduces predation pressure, but it also carries ecological costs through lower prey growth and lower predator conversion opportunity. Indirect evolutionary rescue of the predator can occur when increased predator mortality lowers predator pressure, selection favors reduced prey defense, and the resulting lower-defense prey state improves predator recovery \citep{YamamichiMiner2015}.

This mechanism cannot be tested in a fixed-defense model. A fixed-defense spatial predator--prey model can ask whether spatial patterning alone creates predator persistence beyond a well-mixed reference, but it cannot test evolutionary rescue because the prey defense state does not change. The eco-evolutionary model therefore introduces a defense frequency \(q\), with \(q=0\) representing fully undefended prey and \(q=1\) representing fully defended prey.

Comparing well-mixed and spatial models is useful because diffusion can alter local predator--prey overlap, transient recovery paths, and the basins of attraction of persistent or extinct states. Predator--prey systems are also a central setting in which rapid evolution and ecological dynamics can interact on similar timescales \citep{YoshidaEtAl2003,KasadaYamamichiYoshida2014}. A scalar mortality threshold \(m_c\) is a natural first diagnostic: below the threshold the predator persists, while above it the predator fails to persist. This diagnostic is appropriate when the stress response is monotone and when persistence is controlled by one attracting outcome. It can fail in bistable systems, where persistent and extinct states are both reachable at the same stress.

This report distinguishes spatially extended dynamics from genuinely spatial-pattern-mediated dynamics. A PDE can preserve basin-dependent outcomes without those basins being generated by persistent spatial patterning. The main result is therefore two-part: the well-mixed eco-evolutionary model supports indirect evolutionary rescue, and the spatially extended model is not summarized by a single persistence threshold, but the current mechanism diagnosis is reaction-dominated homogeneous multistability rather than spatial-pattern-mediated rescue.

\subsection{Relation to previous eco-evolutionary rescue models}

This report follows the conceptual structure of indirect evolutionary rescue models in which ecological persistence depends on evolutionary change in another component of the community. Here the evolutionary coordinate is prey defense frequency. Predator mortality stress changes predator pressure; predator pressure changes selection on defense; and the resulting defense state feeds back on prey growth, prey palatability, and predator conversion.

The defense trade-off is intentionally minimal: defended prey have lower growth and lower vulnerability or conversion value than undefended prey. Such defense-growth and defense-palatability trade-offs are enough to create a feedback in which a stressed predator may recover indirectly when prey defense declines \citep{YamamichiMiner2015}. The form of these trade-offs can qualitatively alter eco-evolutionary predator--prey dynamics \citep{KasadaYamamichiYoshida2014}.

The present model is closest in spirit to indirect evolutionary rescue models in which predator mortality stress changes selection on prey defense. In the well-mixed case, the mechanism matches the qualitative logic proposed by \citet{YamamichiMiner2015}: predator decline reduces predation pressure, lower predation pressure favors less-defended prey, and the resulting change in prey state improves predator persistence. The spatial extension studied here asks whether this mechanism remains describable by a mortality threshold once diffusion and spatial initial-condition dependence are introduced.

The contribution of the present report is mathematical and numerical rather than a final biological generalization. It shows that adding spatial dynamics changes the rescue question from a scalar threshold comparison to a basin-structured dynamical problem in the tested parameterization.

\section{Model formulation}

The eco-evolutionary variables are total prey density \(n\), predator density \(w\), prey defense frequency \(q\), free space \(z\), and predator mortality stress \(s\). Free space is
\[
z = \kappa^{-1} - n - w.
\]
The defense trade-off functions are
\[
r(q)=r_u(1-q)+r_v q,
\]
\[
a(q)=a_u(1-q)+a_v q,
\]
\[
b(q)=b_u(1-q)+b_v q.
\]
The well-mixed eco-evolutionary ODE is
\begin{align}
\frac{dn}{dt} &= n[r(q)z-\xi-a(q)w],\\
\frac{dw}{dt} &= w[b(q)nz-(m+s)-\mu w],\\
\frac{dq}{dt} &= \nu q(1-q)[(r_v-r_u)z-(a_v-a_u)w].
\end{align}
The spatial extension uses the same local reaction terms and adds diffusion:
\begin{align}
\partial_t n &= D_n\nabla^2 n + n[r(q)z-\xi-a(q)w],\\
\partial_t w &= D_w\nabla^2 w + w[b(q)nz-(m+s)-\mu w],\\
\partial_t q &= D_q\nabla^2 q + \nu q(1-q)[(r_v-r_u)z-(a_v-a_u)w].
\end{align}
The spatial simulations use zero-flux Neumann boundary conditions.

\section{Numerical questions and diagnostic criteria}

The first numerical question was whether prey defense evolution increases the predator mortality stress tolerated by the well-mixed system. This was measured by comparing the no-evolution and evolution thresholds
\[
m_c^{ODE,\mathrm{no\ evo}}, \quad m_c^{ODE,\mathrm{evo}},
\]
and their difference
\[
\Delta_{evo}^{ODE}=m_c^{ODE,\mathrm{evo}}-m_c^{ODE,\mathrm{no\ evo}}.
\]
A positive value indicates that prey evolution expands the predator persistence range in the well-mixed model.

The second numerical question was whether adding spatial diffusion changes the same rescue effect. A scalar PDE threshold was initially tested because it gives a direct comparison with the ODE threshold when persistence changes monotonically with stress. The later spatial diagnostics were needed because the apparent PDE boundary was sensitive to the tail window, integration horizon, and tail-slope rule. Multi-horizon analysis showed long transients near the apparent boundary, continuation experiments showed direction-dependent outcomes, and basin scans showed that different initial conditions can reach different outcomes at the same stress.

The outcome classes used in the spatial interpretation are scientific rather than procedural. A persistent outcome has a predator density tail that remains positive and steady enough to support persistence. An extinct outcome has predator density near zero. A transient outcome has not settled clearly into either class at the tested horizon. A bistable stress is one where both persistent and extinct outcomes are reachable from different initial states or continuation paths.

\subsection{Numerical protocol}

Well-mixed ODE thresholds were estimated by stress continuation and bisection, with predator persistence assessed from tail behavior. PDE scalar thresholds were used only as preliminary screening quantities because a scalar threshold is meaningful only if stress response is effectively monotone.

Spatial simulations used zero-flux boundaries and diffusion in \(n\), \(w\), and \(q\). Initial PDE persistence calls were based on final-time and tail-window behavior, but sensitivity to tail window, integration horizon, and tail-slope rule motivated multi-horizon diagnostics. Continuation tests then used final states from neighboring stress values as initial states to test path dependence. Basin scans first varied broad initial-condition families and later varied \(q_0\) and \(w_0/w_{\mathrm{baseline}}\) directly. PDE outcomes were interpreted as persistent, extinct, transient, or bistable using tail behavior, steady-state residual checks, and whether multiple outcomes were reachable at the same stress.

\section{Fixed-defense spatial model as a control}

Before introducing prey evolution, the fixed-defense spatial model was used as a control for spatial patterning alone. The question was whether spatial heterogeneity could create predator persistence beyond the well-mixed reference when prey defense was held fixed.

The fixed-defense spatial model did not produce robust ODE-extinct/PDE-persistent predator rescue. The strongest weak candidate occurred at \(D_w/D_u=150\), but further grid and mechanism checks classified it as a transient or numerical candidate rather than a robust spatial rescue mechanism. This control establishes that fixed spatial patterning alone was insufficient for predator rescue in the tested model.

\section{Indirect evolutionary rescue in the well-mixed model}

The well-mixed eco-evolutionary model gave the clean positive result. The no-evolution predator mortality stress threshold was
\[
m_c^{ODE,\mathrm{no\ evo}} = 0.069448242,
\]
whereas the evolving-defense threshold was
\[
m_c^{ODE,\mathrm{evo}} = 0.16486816.
\]
Thus
\[
\Delta_{evo}^{ODE}=0.095419922.
\]
In the rescue window, defense frequency declined from approximately \(0.6726\) to approximately \(0.3336\).

Mechanistically, increased predator mortality reduces predator pressure. Lower predator pressure makes defense less favorable because the growth cost of defense is no longer offset by strong predation avoidance. As \(q\) declines, prey growth and predator conversion opportunity increase, allowing predator persistence over a larger stress interval. This is the cleanest evidence for indirect evolutionary rescue in the model sequence and follows the same qualitative feedback identified by \citet{YamamichiMiner2015}.

\section{Spatial eco-evolutionary model}

The spatial eco-evolutionary model adds diffusion of \(n\), \(w\), and \(q\) to the same local eco-evolutionary reactions. The first scalar-threshold screen suggested that the PDE tolerated less mortality stress than the well-mixed evolving system. The representative screening values were \(m_c^{PDE,\mathrm{no\ evo}}=0.06921875\) and \(m_c^{PDE,\mathrm{evo}}=0.11765625\). Because \(0.11765625 < 0.16486816\), this preliminary comparison suggested a lower PDE-evolution persistence boundary than the ODE-evolution threshold.

That comparison was useful as a warning signal, but it was not a stable final summary of the spatial system. The spatial model showed sensitivity to persistence criteria, long transients, and path-dependent outcomes. These features change the scientific question from ``What is the PDE threshold?'' to ``Which persistent, extinct, or transient outcomes are reachable at a given stress?''

\section{Evidence for path dependence and bistability}

The spatial diagnostics proceeded through four conceptual phases. First, persistence classification was found to depend on the tail window and integration horizon. Second, longer multi-horizon analysis showed that several stresses near the apparent boundary remained transient rather than cleanly persistent or extinct. Third, continuation experiments showed direction-dependent outcomes: upward continuation could maintain a predator-persistent branch at stresses where downward continuation from high stress could remain extinct or transient. Finally, initial-condition basin scans confirmed that predator-persistent and predator-extinct outcomes can both be reached at the same stress.

\section[Initial-defense and predator-abundance basin dependence]{Basin-boundary dependence on \(q_0\) and \(w_0\)}

The focused basin-boundary scan varied initial defense frequency \(q_0\) and initial predator abundance scale \(w_0/w_{\mathrm{baseline}}\) at two stresses inside the bistable interval. At stress \(0.1584375\), the \(q_0\)--\(w_0\) grid gave 20 persistent, 7 extinct, and 43 transient outcomes. At stress \(0.16486816\), it gave 14 persistent, 21 extinct, and 35 transient outcomes. No unresolved or nonphysical initial-condition outcomes were recorded in these two focused grids.

The scan shows that basin entry is two-dimensional. Low-to-intermediate \(q_0\) values tend to favor predator-persistent outcomes, so the initial defense state is a major basin-entry coordinate. Predator initial abundance modulates the outcome within that \(q_0\) range. Transient outcomes remain common, which means the separatrix is not yet fully resolved at the tested horizon.

\section{Mechanism diagnosis: homogeneous reaction dynamics versus spatial patterning}

The basin-boundary scan established that the spatially extended PDE preserves multiple reachable outcomes, but it did not by itself identify whether spatial patterning generates those outcomes. A subsequent matched ODE-PDE mechanism test compared representative PDE spatial means with ODE trajectories from the same initial means, quantified representative spatial variance, compared ODE and PDE basin labels on the same \(q_0\)--\(w_0\) grid, and ran targeted perturbation-sensitivity tests.

The mechanism diagnosis was \texttt{reaction\_dominated\_homogeneous\_multistability}. Representative ODE-PDE classifications agreed in 3 of 3 cases, basin-grid labels agreed for 90\% of 140 \(q_0\)--\(w_0\) points, all 14 disagreements involved transient labels, direct persistent/extinct disagreements were zero, final spatial CV values were very small, and perturbation tests produced no outcome changes. Thus the current evidence indicates that the observed PDE basin structure is mostly inherited from the homogeneous eco-evolutionary reaction dynamics rather than generated by persistent spatial patterning.

The follow-up ODE analysis therefore treats the homogeneous reaction system as the next object of study. The representative ODE trajectories track predator density, defense frequency, the selection gradient
\[
(r_v-r_u)z-(a_v-a_u)w,
\]
and the predator growth factor
\[
b(q)nz-(m+s)-\mu w.
\]
The ODE \(q_0\)--\(w_0\) basin map reproduces persistent, extinct, and transient regions on the same focused grid, and the numerical equilibrium scan gives parameter-specific evidence for multiple homogeneous attractors. These diagnostics support the conditional interpretation that basin-dependent rescue is controlled by homogeneous eco-evolutionary feedbacks under the tested trade-off values.

The equilibrium scan suggests that the persistent homogeneous branch compensates increased predator mortality by lowering the equilibrium defense frequency \(q^*\), while \(n^*\) and \(w^*\) remain close to the same positive values over the tested stress range. This provides a reaction-level explanation for the rescue mechanism and for why the spatial PDE can inherit basin-dependent outcomes without persistent spatial patterning.

\subsection{Homogeneous compensation branch}

The homogeneous compensation branch can be derived directly from the interior ODE equilibrium equations. Let \(c=(r_v-r_u)/(a_v-a_u)\). The selection-gradient condition gives \(w=cz\). With the linear trade-offs, \(r(q)-ca(q)=r_u-ca_u\), so
\[
z^*=\frac{\xi}{r_u-ca_u},\qquad
w^*=cz^*,\qquad
n^*=\kappa^{-1}-z^*-w^*.
\]
The predator balance then determines the required conversion value
\[
b_{\mathrm{req}}(s)=\frac{m+s+\mu w^*}{n^*z^*},
\]
and the conversion trade-off gives
\[
q^*(s)=\frac{b_{\mathrm{req}}(s)-b_u}{b_v-b_u}.
\]
For the current parameterization, this analytic branch matches the numerical stable persistent equilibria to roundoff precision, with maximum \(|\Delta q^*|<10^{-14}\). Along the branch, \(q^*\) decreases as stress increases while \(n^*\) and \(w^*\) remain approximately fixed. A local structured trade-off check found the compensation branch in 72 of 81 nearby parameter sets, so the mechanism is supported locally but should not be treated as globally general.

\paragraph{Interior compensation branch.}
For the linear trade-off ODE, an interior persistent equilibrium with \(n^*>0\), \(w^*>0\), and \(0<q^*<1\) must satisfy
\[
w^*=c z^*,\qquad c=\frac{r_v-r_u}{a_v-a_u}.
\]
When \(c>0\), \(r_u-ca_u>0\), and the resulting
\[
z^*=\frac{\xi}{r_u-ca_u},\qquad
w^*=cz^*,\qquad
n^*=\kappa^{-1}-z^*-w^*
\]
are positive, the predator equation determines
\[
q^*(s)=\frac{(m+s+\mu w^*)/(n^*z^*)-b_u}{b_v-b_u}.
\]
The compensation branch is interior for stresses satisfying \(0<q^*(s)<1\). For the current parameterization, this analytic branch matches the numerical stable persistent equilibria up to machine precision. The analytic Jacobian is locally stable at the tested target stresses, and the local condition grid identifies a nontrivial stable region. These are conditional results for the tested linear trade-offs, not a global theorem for all defense trade-off forms.

The local stability of the interior compensation branch was also evaluated using the Routh--Hurwitz conditions for the cubic characteristic polynomial of the analytic Jacobian. For a polynomial \(\lambda^3+A_1\lambda^2+A_2\lambda+A_3\), local stability requires \(A_1>0\), \(A_2>0\), \(A_3>0\), and \(A_1A_2>A_3\). In the current parameterization, these inequalities hold at the target stresses along the compensation branch and agree with the analytic eigenvalue calculation (Figures~\ref{fig:routh-hurwitz-terms} and~\ref{fig:routh-hurwitz-margin}).

\section{Discussion}

The fixed-defense control shows that spatial patterning alone was insufficient for robust predator rescue in the tested Roy-style model. The well-mixed eco-evolutionary model then shows that evolutionary change in prey defense can rescue predators under mortality stress, consistent with indirect evolutionary rescue theory \citep{YamamichiMiner2015}. The spatially extended eco-evolutionary model preserves basin-dependent outcomes, but the current mechanism audit indicates that those basins are primarily inherited from homogeneous reaction dynamics.

The PDE result is not a simple amplification or suppression of the well-mixed rescue threshold; it is a spatially extended expression of basin-dependent dynamics. This changes what ``spatial effect on rescue'' means. Rather than asking whether the PDE threshold is higher or lower than the ODE threshold, the relevant question is which outcomes are reachable at each stress, how the basins depend on initial ecological and evolutionary state, and whether those basins are generated by homogeneous reactions or by persistent spatial patterning.

The basin-boundary results indicate that initial defense frequency and predator abundance are important coordinates for basin entry. Low-to-intermediate defense frequencies more often reach predator persistence, while high-defense states and some low-predator states more often lead to extinction or unresolved transients. Because transient outcomes remain common, the current basin map should be read as a resolved first layer of basin structure, not as a fully refined separatrix.

\subsection{Strength of current claims}

Strongly supported in the tested parameterization:
\begin{itemize}
  \item well-mixed prey defense evolution increases predator mortality tolerance;
  \item fixed-defense spatial patterning did not produce robust predator rescue;
  \item the spatially extended eco-evolutionary model preserves basin-dependent outcomes in the tested stress range;
  \item the current mechanism diagnosis is reaction-dominated homogeneous multistability rather than spatial-pattern-mediated rescue.
\end{itemize}

Moderately supported:
\begin{itemize}
  \item initial defense frequency \(q_0\) and predator abundance \(w_0\) influence basin entry.
\end{itemize}

Unresolved:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the exact separatrix in \(q_0\)--\(w_0\) space;
  \item sensitivity to diffusion coefficients;
  \item generality across trade-off forms and parameterizations;
  \item analytical characterization of the homogeneous equilibrium and basin structure;
  \item biological calibration.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and future work}

The current analysis has several limitations. It uses one trade-off parameterization, with \(b_u=0.08\) and \(b_v=0.02\). Transient outcomes remain common near the basin boundary. The \(q_0\)--\(w_0\) basin boundary has not yet been adaptively refined. Sensitivity to diffusion coefficients has not yet been tested after basin mapping. The model has also not been externally calibrated to a particular biological system.

The next numerical task is to test how the homogeneous basin structure depends on the defense trade-off parameters and to refine the analytical equilibrium and stability interpretation. Further PDE basin refinement may be useful later, but it should follow the current mechanism correction rather than replace it.

In the well-mixed system, prey defense evolution supports indirect evolutionary rescue. In the spatially extended system, the same eco-evolutionary reaction mechanism does not reduce to a single persistence threshold. Instead, the PDE preserves basin-dependent outcomes, with basin entry controlled in part by initial prey defense frequency and predator abundance. Persistent spatial patterning is not currently supported as the mechanism in this parameterization.
